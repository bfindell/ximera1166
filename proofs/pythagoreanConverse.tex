\begin{abstract}
  We reason about the converse of the Pythagorean Theorem.
\end{abstract}
\maketitle


\begin{problem}

Suppose a triangle has side lengths $a$, $b$, and $c$.  

Pythagorean Theorem: If the triangle is a $\answer[format=string]{right}$ triangle with hypotenuse $c$, then $a^2+b^2=\answer{c^2}$.  

The converse of a true theorem is \wordChoice{\choice{always true}\choice[correct]{sometimes true}\choice{never true}}.  

Converse of the Pythagorean Theorem:  If $a^2+b^2=c^2$, then the triangle is a right triangle with hypotenuse $c$.  

\end{problem}

\begin{problem}

Suppose $a^2+b^2=c^2$.  We want to show that the triangle is a right triangle with hypotenuse $c$.  Until we should that $\angle C$ is a right angle, we must consider the possibility that it is not.  

Construct another triangle: $\triangle XYZ$ with right angle $Z$ and legs of length $a$ and $b$, as shown.  Opposite the $\angle Z$ is a hypotenuse whose length is not yet known, so let's call it $d$.

From (the forward version of) the Pythagorean Theorem, it must follows that $a^2+b^2=d^2$, where $d$ is the hypotenuse of the triangle known to be right.  From the two equations, it must follow that $d^2=c^2$.  Algebraically, we can conclude that $d=\pm c$, which implies that $d=c$ because the side lengths must be positive.   

Now we can conclude that $\triangle ABC \cong \triangle XYZ$ by SSS.  And from the congruent triangles, we know that $\angle C\cong\angle Z$, which means that the $\triangle ABC$ is a right triangle.
